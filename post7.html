Agamben vs. Foucault (Part 2)

Posted by Theory & Philosophy | July 30, 2025

(This is part 2 of a 3-part imaginative reconstruction of a blog post based on the YouTube video "Giorgio Agamben vs. Michel Foucault" from the Theory & Philosophy channel. The original video can be found at https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=4ekPUzWNbcU&t=399s.)

To read the manuscript Mind, Machine, and Will is to be confronted with a peculiar sensation: that of witnessing the meticulous, even cheerful, construction of a prison that presents itself as a paradise of reason. The authors, Daniel Plate and James Hutson, offer us what they believe to be a liberation. By banishing the "Cartesian ghost" of the inner self and grounding all meaning, agency, and law in the observable world of "public practice," they promise a future free from metaphysical illusions. They announce a "revolution" that will resolve the crises of our age—the confusion sown by artificial intelligence, the challenge to free will by neuroscience, and the instability of authorship in law.

Their solution is elegant, comprehensive, and profoundly mistaken. It is not mistaken in its particulars—the synthesis of legal theory, neuroscience, and philosophy is often astute. It is mistaken in its fundamental premise. The authors believe they are witnessing and assisting in a historical transformation of power, a shift from a world of inner states to one of outer practices. They have made the quintessential Foucauldian error: they see a rupture, a new paradigm, a clever evolution in the techniques of governance.

They fail to see that what they are describing is not new at all. It is, in fact, the oldest and most fundamental gesture of Western power, now perfected in a new, technological form. The world they describe is not a "post-Cartesian" utopia; it is the realization of a biopolitical project that has been at the heart of our tradition from the very beginning. What this manuscript provides is not a guide to living after the Cartesian ghost, but a blueprint for the ultimate camp—a zone of indistinction where human life is perfectly and absolutely captured by a new kind of sovereign power. To understand this, we must begin at the beginning. Not with Descartes, but with the Greeks.

The Original Division: Zoe and Bios

The central error of the manuscript, and indeed of the entire philosophical lineage it follows, is its failure to interrogate the meaning of "life" itself. Our tradition, since its inception, has been founded on a crucial but often hidden distinction, one articulated by the ancient Greeks. They had two words for what we call life: zoe and bios.

Zoe was the simple fact of being alive, the bare, natural life common to all living beings—humans, animals, even gods. It is the life of the body, of birth and death, of biological processes.

Bios, in contrast, was the qualified life, the form or way of living proper to an individual or a group. It was the life of the citizen, a political existence, a life that had meaning and form within the polis.

The entire history of Western politics is the history of this division. Politics, for us, has always been the process by which zoe, bare natural life, is captured, excluded, and simultaneously included within the political order, transformed into the subject of bios. And the fundamental act of sovereign power is precisely the power to decide where this line is drawn—to strip bios from a living being, leaving only zoe, a "bare life" that can be disposed of without it counting as a political or religious crime.

Now, let us turn to the world of Mind, Machine, and Will. The authors’ entire argument rests on this very division. The neuroscientific determinism they champion, particularly through the work of Robert Sapolsky, is an operation that reduces the human being to zoe. It tells us that we are nothing more than a "seamless causal chain," a product of our genes, our hormones, our environment. The human being, in this view, is a biological automaton. This is the reduction to bare, natural life.

What, then, of agency and responsibility? These, the authors tell us, are found in "public practice." This is the realm of bios. The biological machine (zoe) is granted a qualified, political life (bios) on the condition that it participates competently in the "language games" of the community. It can be an "agent," it can be held "responsible," not because of any innate freedom, but because the community attributes these qualities to its performance.

Do you see the operation? It is the original biopolitical gesture. Bare life (zoe) is brought into the political order (bios), but only as an object of control. The manuscript does not escape this logic; it is its most perfect expression.

The Persistence of the Sovereign

The authors, following a certain reading of Foucault, believe that sovereign power—the power to "take life or let live"—has been replaced by a new form of power that "fosters life." This is the grand illusion. The sovereign has never disappeared; it has simply changed its form. The supreme power is no longer located in the person of the king, but it has not vanished. It remains as the power that decides on the exception, the power that can suspend the law and produce bare life.

In the world of Mind, Machine, and Will, who is the sovereign? It is the anonymous, distributed power of the "community" and its "practices." It is the system of norms, the legal frameworks, and, most importantly, the algorithmic apparatuses that now govern our lives. This new sovereign does not wield an axe, but it still holds the power of life and death—not necessarily biological death, but political death.

The system decides who is "competent" to participate in its practices. And what of the one who is deemed "incompetent"? What of the one whose performance does not align with the "community standards"? They are not punished in the old sense. Retribution, the authors tell us, is a relic. Instead, they are subject to "reconstruction." They are managed, treated, and rehabilitated. Their bios, their political life, is suspended. They are reduced to their zoe, a patient to be administered, a problem to be solved.

This is the modern form of the sovereign ban. The individual is not cast out of society, but is included within it through their very exclusion. They are the new homo sacer—the one who can be subjected to the full force of the system's therapeutic, corrective power, whose life is entirely at the disposal of the administration, without any recourse to a political status that has been stripped from them. The authors see this as a humane alternative to blame, but it is in fact a more total form of power, for it leaves no space outside of its administrative grasp.

The Algorithmic Camp

This brings us to the final, and most terrifying, implication of the manuscript’s vision. If the state of exception is the paradigm of modern governance, then the camp is the space in which this exception is realized. The camp is the zone where law is suspended, and where individuals are reduced to bare life, subject to the absolute power of the sovereign.

The world described in Mind, Machine, and Will is a new, and perhaps final, form of the camp. It is a camp without walls, a camp in the cloud. It is a space of absolute transparency and total traceability, where every action, every choice, every "practice" is rendered visible and subject to judgment. The traditional distinction between the private and the public, which once offered a sliver of protection from the sovereign’s gaze, is completely obliterated.

In this algorithmic camp, we are all inmates. Our performance is constantly monitored, our competence is perpetually assessed, and our value is calculated by the system. The legal frameworks for copyright, which now judge "transformative use" rather than inner creativity, and the "ethical AI" that nudges our behavior toward "value alignment," are the administrative bylaws of this new camp. They are the mechanisms by which our life is managed and controlled.

The authors believe that this turn to "public practice" ensures fairness and accountability. But this is the logic of the camp administrator who insists that the rules of the camp are, in their own way, just. The problem is not whether the rules are applied fairly, but that we are living in a space where our very being has been stripped of its political form, where we are reduced to the status of manageable life.

The authors have mistaken the perfection of control for the achievement of justice. They have looked upon the face of the most absolute biopolitical apparatus ever conceived and called it a revolution.

The Inoperative Machine

What, then, is to be done? The manuscript offers us a future of better practices, more transparent systems, and more effective forms of communal negotiation. It invites us to become better collaborators in the administration of our own lives. This we must refuse.

The task is not to design a more "ethical AI," for this is merely to design a more efficient shepherd. The task is not to create more "inclusive practices," for this is merely to expand the reach of the camp. The task is to render the entire machine inoperative.

The challenge posed by the convergence of neuroscience, AI, and law is not to find a new way to ground our concepts of agency and responsibility. The challenge is to find a "form-of-life" that can no longer be captured by the distinction between zoe and bios. It is to think a human existence that is not reducible to either bare, biological fact or to a qualified, political status.

This means a fundamental refusal of the terms of the debate. We must not seek to preserve some residue of "inner freedom," for this is the ghost the authors are right to exorcise. But we must also refuse to be defined by our "outer performance," for this is the trap they have laid for us.

The revolution that is truly needed is not a revolution in our theories of mind and law. It is a revolution in our very form-of-life. It is the difficult, patient, and urgent task of finding a way to live that cannot be governed, a way to act that cannot be measured, and a way to be together that cannot be administered. It is only by rendering the biopolitical machine inoperative that we might, for the first time, truly begin to live.
