The Subject in Question: Foucault's Consistent Critique and Its Contradictory Reception

Introduction: The Foucauldian Method and Its Modern Paradox

Michel Foucault remains a titan of critical thought precisely because of the consistency of his method. Across his diverse historical studies, from the asylum to the prison to the clinic, he relentlessly pursued a single project: to conduct a "genealogy of the modern soul" by demonstrating how the human subject is not a natural, pre-existing entity but a historical construct, an effect of specific and contingent systems of power and knowledge. He sought to exorcise the "Cartesian ghost" from our understanding of human life, showing that our innermost selves—our desires, identities, and agency—are produced by external, social, and institutional forces.

Two of his most influential analyses, the study of the "delinquent" in Discipline and Punish and the "author" in "What is an Author?", perfectly exemplify this consistent approach. In both, he deconstructs a seemingly natural category of personhood, revealing it to be a "function" of discourse that serves to organize, regulate, and control society. Yet, the reception of these parallel critiques presents a profound paradox, particularly in progressive and academic circles. Foucault's analysis of the criminal is often readily accepted; students and scholars alike are receptive to the idea that the "delinquent" is a social construct, a product of a carceral system that creates the very object it purports to manage. This view aligns comfortably with a compassionate, systemic critique of the justice system.

However, when this same critical lens is turned upon the author, a significant resistance emerges. The idea that the "author" is also a social construct—a regulatory function designed to limit and classify meaning—is often met with skepticism or outright rejection. This document will explore this asymmetrical reception. It will first detail Foucault's remarkably consistent deconstruction of the subject in both criminality and authorship. It will then investigate why we are so willing to accept a Foucauldian analysis of the person we blame, but so reluctant to apply it to the person we praise. The answer, it will be argued, lies in a deep cultural and psychological attachment to the Romantic myth of the artist-genius, an ideal that underpins our entire economy of merit, praise, and cultural value.

Part I: The Subject as a Function of Power/Knowledge

Foucault’s central thesis is that power and knowledge are inextricably linked; they are two sides of the same coin in the process of "subjectification". Power is not merely repressive; it is productive. It produces reality, domains of objects, and rituals of truth. It produces the very individuals it seeks to govern. This method is applied with remarkable consistency to both the figure of the criminal and the figure of the author.

Section 1: The Making of the Delinquent in Discipline and Punish

In Discipline and Punish, Foucault charts the historical shift in Western penal systems from a logic of spectacular, public torture aimed at the body to a logic of subtle, pervasive discipline aimed at the "soul". This was not, he argues, a story of humanitarian progress. Rather, it was a refinement in the technology of power, making it more efficient, economical, and total.

The key to this new mode of power is "discipline," a set of techniques for controlling the body in space and time to render it both useful and docile. Through hierarchical observation, normalizing judgment, and the examination, institutions like the prison, the school, and the hospital began to produce "docile bodies"—individuals shaped and molded to conform to societal norms. The Panopticon, Jeremy Bentham's architectural design for a prison where inmates are under constant potential surveillance, serves as Foucault's ultimate metaphor for this disciplinary society. The inmate, never knowing if they are being watched, internalizes the gaze of power and begins to police themselves.

Crucially, this new system of power/knowledge, particularly through the "human sciences" of criminology and psychiatry, did not simply punish crime; it produced the "delinquent". The focus of the justice system shifted from the criminal act to the criminal's character, motives, and psychology—their "soul". The delinquent is thus a social and scientific construct, an object of knowledge created by the very institutions that claim to study and reform them. The criminal is no longer simply one who breaks the law, but a specific type of person, an "abnormal" individual to be managed, classified, and controlled. In this analysis, the criminal subject with a "free" and malicious will is dissolved, replaced by an individual produced at the intersection of discursive and institutional power.

Section 2: The Invention of the Author in "What is an Author?"

Foucault applies the same deconstructive logic to the figure of the author. In his 1969 essay, he argues that the "author" is not the flesh-and-blood individual who writes a text, but rather an "author function"—a social construct that emerged at a specific historical moment to serve particular regulatory needs. The author, he argues, is a fiction invented by critics and institutions to provide a unifying principle that limits a text's infinite meanings.

Foucault identifies four key characteristics of this function:

  • It is tied to legal and institutional systems. The author function emerged when texts became objects of property and when writers could be punished for transgressive discourse. The author's name makes someone legally responsible for a text.
  • It does not operate uniformly across all discourses. A scientific paper and a novel have authors in very different ways. For centuries, literary texts were anonymous, while scientific texts required an author's name to guarantee their truthfulness. In the modern era, this has reversed.
  • It is not a spontaneous attribution but a complex construction. We construct the "author" by making comparisons between texts, identifying coherent themes, and establishing a uniform style. This constructed figure is a projection of our way of handling texts, not a discovery of the person behind them.
  • It does not refer to a single individual but gives rise to multiple "egos." The "I" who speaks in a novel's preface is different from the "I" of the narrator. The author function creates a series of subjective positions that can be occupied, rather than referring to the singular consciousness of the writer.

For Foucault, the traditional idea of the author as a "genial creator"—a solitary figure of infinite expression—is a Romantic myth. He argues that writing is an "interplay of signs" where the "writing subject endlessly disappears". The author function serves to contain this disappearance, to give a chaotic proliferation of meaning a name, a face, and a limit. Just as the "delinquent" is a product of the carceral system, the "author" is a product of the systems of discourse that regulate knowledge, property, and meaning in our culture.

Part II: The Asymmetrical Embrace of a Deconstructed Subject

Foucault's analysis is consistent: both the criminal and the author are subjects produced by power, not pre-existing essences. Why, then, is one analysis so readily accepted and the other so fiercely resisted?

Section 3: The Political and Ethical Appeal of the Foucauldian Criminal

The deconstruction of the criminal subject is appealing to a modern, progressive sensibility for several reasons. First, it aligns perfectly with a deterministic worldview that sees human behavior as the product of complex social, economic, and biological forces. Foucault's genealogy provides a historical and philosophical framework for the scientific insights of thinkers like Robert Sapolsky, who argue that free will is an illusion and that behavior is determined "all the way down".

Second, this analysis provides a powerful ethical argument for moving from a retributive model of justice to a rehabilitative or reconstructive one. If the "delinquent" is a product of a system, then it is illogical and inhumane to punish them as if they were the sole, free authors of their actions. Instead, the focus should be on reforming the system and rehabilitating the individual. This Foucauldian perspective gives a voice to the excluded and challenges the very legitimacy of the institutions that create and manage them. It is a critique that serves the political goal of social reform and aligns with a compassionate view of human fallibility.

Section 4: The Cultural and Economic Resistance to the Foucauldian Author

The resistance to deconstructing the author-subject is more complex and reveals a deep-seated contradiction in the modern worldview. While we may be willing to absolve individuals of blame, we are far less willing to absolve them of praise.

The Myth of the Genius and the Meritocracy: Our culture remains profoundly attached to the Romantic myth of the artist as a unique genius—a solitary, pioneering, and semi-divine creator. This myth is the cornerstone of our meritocratic ideal, which holds that success is earned through individual talent and effort. Foucault's argument that the author is a social function directly threatens this cherished belief. If the artist is not an originator but a node in a discursive network, then the entire basis for praise, reward, and merit is called into question. This is a far more destabilizing idea than questioning blame, as it challenges the moral justification for the success of our cultural heroes and, by extension, ourselves.

Economic and Institutional Stakes: The "author function" is not just an idea; it is the legal and economic bedrock of the entire creative world. Copyright law, publishing houses, galleries, and museums are all organized around the principle of the author as an originator and owner of property. To accept Foucault's critique in its entirety would be to question the fundamental structures that assign value and distribute capital in the cultural field. While many are willing to critique the prison-industrial complex, far fewer are prepared to critique the "author-industrial complex" from which they derive cultural and often economic benefit.

The Artist as a Figure of Power: Foucault's analysis reveals that the artist is not simply a figure of resistance against power, but is also a product of it. The "author function" is a mechanism of power that classifies and controls discourse. The artist's position within the art world is shaped by a complex network of institutions, markets, and canons that confer legitimacy and value. This is a more difficult truth to accept than the idea of the criminal as a pure victim of power. It suggests that even our figures of supposed freedom and transgression are themselves implicated in and produced by the very systems of power they might seek to critique.

Conclusion: The Challenge of a Consistent Critique

The asymmetrical reception of Foucault's work on criminality and authorship is not an intellectual failure on his part, but a revelation of our own ideological inconsistencies. We readily embrace a systemic, deterministic critique when it serves the compassionate goal of absolving individuals of blame. We resist that same critique when it threatens our deeply held beliefs in individual merit, praise, and the Romantic ideal of the artist as a self-creating genius.

A consistent Foucauldian analysis, however, demands that we apply the same genealogical method to all subjects, whether they are objects of condemnation or celebration. It forces us to recognize that the "delinquent" and the "author" are two sides of the same coin: they are not natural kinds of people but historical constructs, "functions" produced by vast, impersonal networks of power and knowledge. To accept Foucault's ideas about the criminal is to begin the work of dismantling a punitive society. To accept his ideas about the author is to confront the far more unsettling task of dismantling the myths that underpin our own sense of individual worth and cultural value. It is a challenge to see that the systems that produce our failures are the very same systems that produce our triumphs, and that the "Cartesian ghost" must be exorcised from the artist's studio just as surely as it is from the prisoner's cell.
