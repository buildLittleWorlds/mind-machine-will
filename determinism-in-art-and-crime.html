Determinism in Art and Crime

A central contradiction animates modern ethical intuitions: we are increasingly willing to view criminal behavior through a deterministic lens, seeing individuals as products of forces beyond their control, while simultaneously clinging to a meritocratic, libertarian view of artistic creation, where individuals are praised as the unique authors of their genius. This document explores the asymmetry in how we apply determinism to blame and praise. Drawing on the neuroscientific work of Robert Sapolsky and the philosophical critiques of Michel Foucault, it deconstructs the social and historical forces that shape both the "delinquent" and the "author." Ultimately, it proposes a unified, practice-based framework that resolves this contradiction by relocating responsibility from the inaccessible "inner states" of individuals to the public, observable sphere of social practice.

Part I: The Case for Universal Determinism

Section 1: The Determined Criminal

The modern argument against free will is powerfully articulated by neuroscientist Robert Sapolsky, who posits that every human action is the result of an unbroken causal chain, leaving no room for an uncaused choice.[1] His central thesis is that "we are nothing more or less than the cumulative biological and environmental luck, over which we had no control, that has brought us to any moment."[2] This deterministic framework is often visualized with the metaphor of "turtles all the way down," signifying that there are only antecedent causes stretching back in time.[3]

If individuals are not the ultimate authors of their actions, the entire edifice of retributive justice—which is predicated on the idea that people deserve blame and punishment—collapses. In a world without free will, concepts like praise and blame "make no sense whatsoever as virtues."[4] Hating a person for a crime they were determined to commit becomes as irrational as "hating the sky for storming."[5] The necessary alternative is a "public health" or "quarantine" model of justice, which dispenses with moral condemnation and focuses instead on pragmatic, forward-looking goals: preventing future harm and rehabilitating individuals where possible.[6]

Section 2: The Determined Artist

In stark contrast to the criminal, the artist is typically viewed through a libertarian lens as a unique genius deserving of praise and reward. This ideal of the "Romantic author" emerged in the late 18th and early 19th centuries as a reaction against Enlightenment rationalism, championing emotion, imagination, and the subjective self as the ultimate source of creative truth.[7] William Wordsworth, in his preface to Lyrical Ballads, famously defined poetry as "the spontaneous overflow of powerful feelings," arguing that the poet is a "man speaking to men" but one endowed with a "more lively sensibility" and a "more comprehensive soul."[8] This ideology elevates the artist to a quasi-religious figure whose value lies in the authentic expression of their inner states.[9]

However, this myth is philosophically unstable. The concept of "moral luck," developed by philosophers Thomas Nagel and Bernard Williams, reveals that artistic achievement is profoundly shaped by factors beyond an agent's control.[10] Nagel identifies four kinds of luck that apply directly to the artist: constitutive luck (innate talents), circumstantial luck (the environment one is born into), causal luck (the broader problem of determinism), and resultant luck (how one's projects turn out).[11] Williams's famous thought experiment involving the painter Paul Gauguin, who abandons his family for his art, illustrates that the justification for his decision depends entirely on his eventual success—an outcome contingent on luck.[12]

Sociological and economic evidence further supports this deterministic view. Artistic success is not a direct measure of raw talent but is mediated by a powerful institutional system of galleries, museums, and critics.[13] Sir Alan Bowness, a former director of the Tate, proposed a deterministic sequence of "conditions of success" for artists, arguing that fame is predictable and constructed by this system.[14] The artist, like the criminal, is a product of a dense web of causation.

Part II: Foucault's Unified Critique of the Subject

Section 3: The Social Construction of the Delinquent

Michel Foucault's work provides a consistent method for deconstructing the modern subject. In Discipline and Punish, he argues that the modern penal system shifted from public torture aimed at the body to a pervasive discipline aimed at the "soul."[15] This new mode of power, exercised through institutions like the prison and the school, produces "docile bodies"—individuals shaped and molded to conform to societal norms.[16] The key to this system is surveillance, perfectly encapsulated by Jeremy Bentham's Panopticon, a prison design where inmates, under the constant threat of being watched, internalize the gaze of power and begin to police themselves.[17]

Crucially, this system of power/knowledge did not simply punish crime; it produced the "delinquent."[18] The focus shifted from the criminal act to the criminal's character and psychology. The delinquent is thus a social and scientific construct, an "abnormal" individual created by the very institutions that claim to study and reform them.[19]

Section 4: The Invention of the "Author Function"

Foucault applies the same deconstructive logic to the figure of the author. He argues that the "author" is not a real person but an "author function"—a social construct that emerged to serve regulatory needs.[20] This function serves to classify discourse and limit a text's infinite meanings.[21] Foucault identifies four key characteristics of this function: it is tied to legal and institutional systems (making someone responsible for a text); it does not operate uniformly across all discourses; it is a complex social construction, not a spontaneous attribution; and it gives rise to multiple "egos" rather than referring to a single individual.[22] The traditional idea of the author as a "genial creator" is a Romantic myth; in reality, the author function is a mechanism of power that contains the "endless disappearance" of the writing subject.[23]

Part III: The Asymmetry and Its Resolution

Section 5: Why We Accept One and Reject the Other

The asymmetrical reception of these parallel critiques reveals a deep contradiction in the modern worldview. The deconstruction of the criminal subject is appealing to a progressive sensibility because it aligns with a deterministic worldview and provides an ethical argument for a rehabilitative justice system.[24] It is a critique that serves the political goal of social reform.

The resistance to deconstructing the author, however, stems from our profound cultural attachment to the myth of the artist as a unique genius, a cornerstone of our meritocratic ideal.[25] If the artist is not an originator, the entire basis for praise and reward is threatened. Furthermore, the "author function" is the economic and legal bedrock of the creative industries, from copyright law to publishing houses.[26] This critique is far more destabilizing as it challenges the moral justification for the success of our cultural heroes and the economic systems that support them.

This asymmetry also has psychological roots. Research shows that blame and praise are not mirror images; blame judgments tend to be more "extreme" and "differentiated."[27] We scrutinize the agent's control and intentions far more closely for a negative act than for a positive one, a bias likely rooted in the evolutionary need to regulate threats to the community.[28]

Section 6: A Unified, Forward-Looking Ethic

The resolution to this paradox lies in abandoning the backward-looking framework of desert and adopting a consistent, forward-looking, and instrumental view of both praise and blame. This approach, rooted in the philosophy of Ludwig Wittgenstein, rejects the "inner-state" model of agency and relocates meaning in public, social practice.[6] In this framework, we stop asking the metaphysical question, "What does this person truly deserve?" and start asking the pragmatic, social question, "What kind of behavior do we as a community want to encourage or discourage?"

  • For Criminals: We should not blame them in a retributive sense, but we should intervene to protect the community and encourage pro-social behavior. The focus is entirely forward-looking.
  • For Artists: We should not praise them because they metaphysically "deserve" it. We should praise and reward them because doing so incentivizes the creation of art, which our society deems valuable. The system of rewards is a social technology designed to encourage productive behavior.

This unified framework provides a consistent, pragmatic, and philosophically robust answer to the initial challenge, one that retains a place for both compassion in our justice system and celebration in our cultural life, but grounds both in a coherent, unified framework.
