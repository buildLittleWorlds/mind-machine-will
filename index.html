Mind, Machine, and Will: A Symposium of Imagined Voices

A Note on This Project's Fictional Nature

This blog and all its content are created by Daniel Plate, lead author of Mind, Machine, and Will. Each essay is a work of speculative fiction, parody, and critical commentary.

The opinions, arguments, and voices presented in these posts are simulations and do not represent the actual views of the public figures named. Their names and public personas are used here under the principles of parody and transformative use for the sole purpose of exploring the philosophical themes of the manuscript through a variety of critical lenses.

This project is a non-commercial, intellectual exercise. If any individual named in a post feels their persona has been unfairly misrepresented and wishes for the content to be removed, please contact me directly at dplate@lindenwood.edu.

The Manuscript's Thesis

The manuscript Mind, Machine, and Will by Professors Daniel Plate and James Hutson proposes a unified framework to address what they identify as three converging crises of the 21st century: the human authorship crisis in copyright law, the free will crisis in neuroscience, and the understanding crisis in artificial intelligence.

The authors argue that these crises all stem from a single, foundational philosophical error: the reliance on "private mental states"—such as intention, belief, and metaphysical free will—to explain concepts like creativity, choice, and responsibility. They refer to this tradition as the "Cartesian ghost," a legacy of thought that has become untenable in a world of complex technological and social systems.

Drawing heavily on the philosophy of Ludwig Wittgenstein and the neuroscience of Robert Sapolsky, Plate and Hutson advocate for a "practice-centered" alternative. In their view, agency and meaning are not found in an inaccessible inner world, but are publicly observable achievements that emerge from our participation in shared social practices, rules, and institutions. The book applies this framework to reinterpret legal doctrines, reformulate ethical systems for AI, and rethink criminal justice in a determined world.

About This Project

This blog is a literary and philosophical experiment. The central thesis of Mind, Machine, and Will is provocative and has profound implications. If we accept its premises, how does our understanding of art, law, politics, and our own humanity change? What is gained, and what might be lost?

To explore these questions, I have created a collection of imaginary, "what-if" essays from the perspectives of various writers, thinkers, and online creators. Each post is a fictional exercise, an attempt to channel a specific intellectual or cultural viewpoint to stress-test the book's arguments.

How it works: I've selected a range of public figures and archetypal online voices. Based on their existing work and public personas, I've imagined how they might react to the manuscript. I've attempted to capture their unique voice, style, and intellectual preoccupations. These are not real posts by these individuals, but good-faith efforts to simulate a broad and critical public conversation.

The goal is not to endorse any single viewpoint, but to use these imagined responses as a crucible for the book's ideas. The posts themselves—ranging from academic critiques to passionate takedowns—are where the interpretation and debate happen. This project is a fictional exploration of the very real anxieties and possibilities of our determined age.
